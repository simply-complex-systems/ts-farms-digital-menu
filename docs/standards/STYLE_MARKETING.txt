STYLE_MARKETING
Purpose: Attention-economics style foundation. Governs print collateral
and digital promotion -- any context where the document competes for
attention against other stimuli and must convert that attention into
action.
Status: LIVING STANDARD
Created: 2026-04-11
Parent: STANDARD_FORMATTING
Children: None declared.

This file inherits everything from STANDARD_FORMATTING and declares
only what shifts. If a property is not named here, the root governs.

================================================================================
1. PURPOSE
================================================================================

Marketing lives in the tension between attention and trust. The reader
did not ask for this document. It arrived -- in a feed, a mailbox, a
rack, a screen. The document has seconds, not minutes. Every element
either earns continued attention or loses it.

The root constraints still govern. Symmetry is on. Survive is on.
Predictability is on. These are not relaxed for marketing -- they are
the reason marketing works. A chaotic layout signals chaos. A
predictable layout signals competence. Trust is the conversion
mechanism, not cleverness.

This style governs two families:

PRINT COLLATERAL -- flyers, one-pagers, brochures, sell sheets, print
ads, posters. Fixed dimensions. No scroll. No state. The reader sees
the whole surface at once.

DIGITAL PROMOTION -- social media assets, display ads, landing page
heroes, banner graphics. Variable dimensions. May scroll. May animate.
The reader sees a fragment first and decides whether to continue.

Both families share the same hierarchy: hook, proof, action. The
layout differs. The economics do not.

================================================================================
2. TOGGLE POSITIONS
================================================================================

ONE_VOICE -- ON
One typeface. Marketing does not need typographic variety. It needs
typographic authority. Hierarchy is expressed through size, weight,
and case. A second typeface splits attention between two personalities
when the document can only afford one.

ONE_INK -- OFF
Marketing contexts introduce brand colors. Additional colors earn
their place by carrying distinct information:
  -- Primary brand color: one. Used for the action element (CTA) and
     nowhere else. The action is the only element that earns color
     distinction from the ink system.
  -- Secondary brand color: permitted only if it carries a different
     structural role than primary -- e.g., category indicator, accent
     on proof elements. If secondary does the same work as primary,
     it does not enter.
  -- Background surface may shift from white when brand requires.
     Ink color inverts to maintain contrast. TWO colors of ink
     against surface maximum. Ink plus brand-action. No third ink.

Entry rule: a color enters only by carrying information the reader
cannot get from position or typography alone. If the hierarchy works
in black and white, the color is decorative. Decorative color does
not enter.

BREATHE -- FULL
White space is a luxury signal. Dense marketing communicates
desperation -- too much to say, too little confidence in any of it.
Generous spacing communicates authority. The reader trusts what has
room to breathe. The page is unhurried even when the message is
urgent.

EARNED_DISTINCTION -- RELAXED
The root sets this to STRICT. Marketing relaxes it. Limited ornament
is permitted where it serves impact without misleading hierarchy.

What relaxed permits:
  -- Oversized type at Level 1 beyond the root 16pt ceiling when
     the headline is the expressive node (see ONE_EXPRESSIVE_NODE).
  -- A single visual flourish -- a background texture, a geometric
     accent, a brand pattern -- that does not compete with content.
  -- Rounded corners, subtle shadows, or border treatments on the
     CTA element to distinguish it from the content plane.

What relaxed does not permit:
  -- Multiple elements competing for ornamental attention.
  -- Ornament on body text, proof elements, or metadata.
  -- Decorative borders or dividers that do not separate content.
  -- Animation or motion effects on non-action elements (digital).

The test: remove the ornament. Does the hierarchy survive? If yes,
the ornament is earned. If no, the ornament is structural and should
be expressed through the type system instead.

ONE_EXPRESSIVE_NODE -- ON
One element carries personality. Everything else is structure. In
marketing, the expressive node is either:
  -- The hero image, or
  -- The headline.
Never both. If the image is expressive, the headline is structural
(clear, direct, typographically restrained). If the headline is
expressive (large, stylized, personality-forward), the image is
structural (supporting, contextual, restrained).

The expressive node is the entry point. The reader's eye lands here
first. Everything else guides the eye from this point to the action.

================================================================================
3. TYPE SYSTEM SHIFTS
================================================================================

The root defines five levels at 16, 14, 12, 10, 8. Marketing
increases contrast between levels to sharpen hierarchy at a glance.

LEVEL    ROOT    MARKETING    ROLE
  1       16      24-36       Hook. Headline. The entry point.
  2       14      14-16       Subhead. Bridge between hook and proof.
  3       12      12          Proof text. Testimonials. Feature names.
  4       10      10          Body. Supporting detail. Terms.
  5        8       8          Legal. Disclaimer. Fine print.

Level 1 is the only level with a range. The size within that range
is determined by the headline's role:
  -- If headline is the expressive node: upper range (28-36).
  -- If headline is structural (image is expressive): lower range
     (24-28).

The range does not mean the agent chooses freely. The agent selects
the size that fills the available width at a natural line break. A
headline that wraps mid-phrase to hit a target size has failed. The
content determines the size. The size does not determine the content.

Bold usage shifts:
  -- Level 1: bold permitted but not required. The size carries
     sufficient hierarchy signal.
  -- Level 2: bold. Always. The subhead must anchor between the
     headline and body without getting lost.
  -- Level 3: bold on feature names or proof labels only. Body
     proof text is regular.
  -- Levels 4-5: regular. No bold in supporting text.

Uppercase shifts:
  -- Level 2 subheads: uppercase when used as a category label
     (e.g., "FEATURES", "TESTIMONIALS"). Sentence case when used
     as a narrative bridge.
  -- CTA text: uppercase. The action is categorical.

================================================================================
4. PAGE GEOMETRY & LAYOUT
================================================================================

PRINT COLLATERAL

Paper: US Letter default. Other sizes declared per piece.
Margins: root values (top 720, bottom 720, left 1440, right 1440 DXA)
as minimum. Marketing may increase margins. Marketing never decreases
margins. Tighter margins do not create more impact -- they create more
noise.

THE Z-PATTERN

Print collateral follows the Z-pattern -- the natural scan path of a
reader encountering a fixed-dimension page:

  1. TOP-LEFT -- Entry. The eye lands here first. Brand mark or
     headline anchor.
  2. TOP-RIGHT -- Secondary anchor. Date, tagline, or supporting
     identity.
  3. DIAGONAL -- The eye sweeps from top-right to bottom-left,
     crossing the proof zone. Social proof, features, supporting
     evidence sit along or near this diagonal.
  4. BOTTOM-RIGHT -- Terminal. The call to action lives here. The
     eye's natural resting point. The last thing seen is the first
     thing acted on.

The Z-pattern is not a grid -- it is a scan path. The grid supports
it. Elements align to the grid but are prioritized by their position
in the Z-path.

THE HOOK-PROOF-ACTION STACK

The vertical hierarchy for any marketing layout:

  HOOK -- Top third. Headline, hero image, or both. Earns continued
  attention. Answers: "Why should I keep looking?"

  PROOF -- Middle third. Evidence. Testimonials, features, data,
  social proof. Answers: "Why should I believe this?"

  ACTION -- Bottom third. CTA. Contact information. Next step.
  Answers: "What do I do now?"

The thirds are approximate. The hook may take 40% on an image-led
piece. The proof may compress to 20% on a simple offer. But the
sequence is fixed. Hook before proof. Proof before action. No
exceptions.

DIGITAL PROMOTION

Dimensions: declared per asset. No default. Social platforms,
display networks, and landing pages each impose their own viewport.

Single-column for vertical assets (stories, pins). The
hook-proof-action stack maps directly.

For horizontal assets (banners, display): the Z-pattern compresses
to a left-to-right read. Hook left, proof center, action right.

Landing page heroes follow the hook-proof-action stack with scroll
as the transition between zones.

DOMINANT VISUAL PLACEMENT

One image dominates. It occupies the largest visual area in the
layout. It is either the expressive node (carrying personality) or
the proof anchor (demonstrating the product, showing the result).

Placement rules:
  -- The dominant visual touches or crosses the centerline of the
     layout. It is not pushed to a corner.
  -- Text does not overlay the focal point of the image. If text
     must overlay, the image provides a low-contrast region for it
     (natural negative space, gradient overlay, or solid band).
  -- No other image competes in scale. Secondary images, if present,
     are less than 25% of the dominant image's area.

================================================================================
5. VERTICAL SPACING
================================================================================

Root rhythm (1.15 / 276 DXA) holds for body text. Shifts below.

HEADLINE (Level 1):    before 0, after 200
  Space after headline is the largest gap on the page. It
  separates the hook from everything below and signals
  confidence. The headline does not crowd its proof.

SUBHEAD (Level 2):     before 160, after 60
  Bridges hook and proof. Enough space above to separate from
  headline, tight below to bind to proof content.

PROOF ELEMENT:         before 80, after 80
  Even spacing. Proof elements are peers -- testimonials, features,
  data points. Equal spacing signals equal weight.

CTA:                   before 240, after 0
  The largest gap above any element. The action is separated from
  proof by a decisive pause. The reader must cross space to reach
  it. This is intentional. The space is a threshold -- crossing it
  is a micro-commitment.

LEGAL / FINE PRINT:    before 120, after 0
  Separated from CTA. Present but not competing.

Principle: the vertical gaps get larger as the reader moves down
the page. Hook is dense with energy. Proof is evenly spaced. Action
is isolated. Legal is distant. The rhythm decelerates. The reader
arrives at the action with room to decide.

================================================================================
6. STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS
================================================================================

HERO IMAGE / HEADLINE
The expressive node (see toggle). One of these carries personality.
The other is structure. The agent determines which based on the
content: if the image is strong (product shot, result photo, human
face), it leads. If the image is generic or absent, the headline
leads.

SOCIAL PROOF
Placement: middle third, along or near the Z-pattern diagonal.
Forms: testimonial quotes (attributed, real), data points (specific
numbers, not vague claims), client logos (if recognized -- unknown
logos are noise, not proof), case result summaries.

Rules:
  -- Social proof is specific. "Trusted by thousands" is not proof.
     "12,847 customers in 2025" is proof.
  -- Attribution is required. Unattributed quotes are fiction.
  -- Three proof elements maximum. More dilutes. The strongest three
     outperform five adequate ones.

CALL TO ACTION (CTA)
One primary CTA per layout. One. Not two, not three. A secondary
CTA is permitted only if it serves a different audience or a
different commitment level (e.g., "Buy now" primary, "Learn more"
secondary).

Design:
  -- CTA is the only element that earns the primary brand color as
     a fill or border.
  -- CTA text is uppercase, Level 4 size, bold.
  -- CTA has the highest contrast ratio on the page. If the page is
     white, the CTA is dark (or brand-colored) on white. If the
     page is dark, the CTA is light on dark.
  -- CTA does not hide. It is visible without scrolling on digital.
     It is in the bottom-right quadrant on print.
  -- CTA states the action, not the feeling. "Start your trial" not
     "Feel the difference." The reader decides what to feel.

DIVIDERS
Minimal. Marketing uses white space as the primary separator. A
divider appears only between the proof zone and the action zone
if the spacing alone does not create sufficient separation. When
used: single line, 1pt, reduced opacity. Same as root.

COLOR ENTRY
When brand colors enter per ONE_INK OFF:
  -- Declare the palette: primary, secondary (if earned), surface.
  -- Map each color to exactly one structural role.
  -- Test: render the layout in grayscale. If hierarchy collapses,
     color is doing structural work that typography should handle.
     Fix the typography, then restore color.

================================================================================
7. DESIGN HEURISTICS
================================================================================

The agent decision sequence for marketing contexts. Extends root
heuristics, does not replace them.

STEP 1 -- IDENTIFY THE ATTENTION BUDGET
Every element on the page costs attention. The budget is finite and
small -- seconds, not minutes. Before placing any element, ask: does
this earn its cost? If the answer is uncertain, the element does not
appear.

The attention budget is not metaphorical. It is the literal time a
reader spends before deciding to engage or leave. Every element that
does not serve hook, proof, or action is spending budget without
earning return.

STEP 2 -- DETERMINE THE EXPRESSIVE NODE
Image or headline. Not both. The content determines which. If the
agent has a strong image, the image leads. If not, the headline
leads. If neither is strong, the layout has a content problem, not
a design problem. Surface to THE_USER.

STEP 3 -- LAY THE Z-PATH (print) OR STACK (digital)
Place the four Z-path anchors or the three stack zones. Everything
else arranges relative to these. No element floats outside the path.

STEP 4 -- PLACE PROOF IN THE MIDDLE
Three elements maximum. Specific. Attributed. If proof is weak,
the layout will not save it. Surface to THE_USER.

STEP 5 -- ISOLATE THE ACTION
CTA gets the most space above it of any element on the page. It
sits at the terminal point. It is the destination the layout has
been guiding the reader toward.

STEP 6 -- REMOVE
The last step is subtraction. Look at every element. Remove one.
Does the layout still work? If yes, it was not earning its place.
Continue until every remaining element is load-bearing.

ANTI-PATTERNS

EVERYTHING COMPETES -- Multiple headlines, multiple images at equal
scale, multiple CTAs at equal weight. The reader's eye has no path.
Nothing is primary because everything claims to be. Fix: choose one
winner at each level. The rest support.

STOCK PHOTOGRAPHY THAT COMMUNICATES NOTHING -- A smiling person
shaking hands. A laptop on a desk. A city skyline. These images
occupy space without earning it. They cost attention and deliver
no information. Fix: use a product image, a result image, or no
image. An empty space is better than a meaningless one.

CALLS TO ACTION THAT HIDE -- CTA buried in body text. CTA in the
same color as everything else. CTA worded as a suggestion rather
than an action. Fix: the CTA is the highest-contrast element. It
states a verb. It is visually distinct from every other element.

CLEVER COPY THAT OBSCURES THE OFFER -- Headlines that require
decoding. Puns that delay comprehension. Wordplay that prioritizes
the writer's wit over the reader's understanding. Fix: the headline
communicates the offer in the time it takes to read it once. If
rereading is required, rewrite.

URGENCY WITHOUT SUBSTANCE -- "Limited time!" with no deadline.
"Only a few left!" with no number. "Act now!" with no reason.
Manufactured urgency erodes trust. Fix: urgency is specific and
true, or it does not appear. "Offer ends April 30" is urgency.
"Hurry!" is noise.

DENSITY AS THOROUGHNESS -- Filling every inch of the layout because
there is more to say. The reader does not reward thoroughness in
marketing. The reader rewards clarity. Fix: say less. Say it with
space around it. Let the proof do the persuading.
